# Cover Letter Template

**Purpose:** Professional middle-ground template - sounds human, not AI-written or too casual

**Last Updated:** [DATE]
**Based on:** ExampleCompany Director IT cover letter (successful version)

---

## Template Structure

### Header (Standard)
```
[Your Name]
[City, State ZIP]
[Phone]
[Email]
[LinkedIn URL]

[Date]

Hiring Manager
[Company Name]
[Location/Remote]

Dear Hiring Manager,
```

### Paragraph 1: Opening Hook (2-3 sentences)
**Purpose:** Why this specific role caught your attention

**Formula:**
- State the role title and company
- Mention specific aspect that aligns with your approach
- Brief credibility statement (years of experience)

**Example:**
> The [Job Title] role at [Company] caught my attention because it's looking for someone who [key requirement from job posting]. That has been my approach to [field] leadership for the past [X] years.

**Tips:**
- Be specific about what caught your attention
- Avoid: "I'm excited", "I'm thrilled", "for a specific reason"
- Use: "caught my attention because", "aligns with", "matches"

---

### Paragraph 2: Current Role Context (3-4 sentences)
**Purpose:** Establish your current environment and relevant experience

**Formula:**
- State current company and what they do
- Address any gaps proactively (e.g., "While not technically [X], the operations are similar")
- List 2-3 major technical achievements relevant to target role
- Use past tense for completed work: "I have built", "I have implemented"

**Example:**
> I currently lead [function] for [Company], where [brief company context]. While not technically [potential gap], the operations are similar: [how it's similar]. I have [achievement 1], [achievement 2], and [achievement 3].

**Tips:**
- Use "I have [past tense]" for formal tone (not "I've")
- Keep technical details specific but not overwhelming
- Bridge any obvious gaps early

---

### Paragraph 3: Strategic + Tactical Balance (3-4 sentences)
**Purpose:** Show you operate at both levels

**Formula:**
- Acknowledge the role requires both strategic and hands-on work
- Give specific examples of each
- Include insight about why staying technical matters

**Example:**
> My current role requires balancing strategic and tactical work. In practice, that means I spend time [hands-on examples], alongside [strategic examples]. I've found that staying technical helps me [benefit of staying technical].

**Tips:**
- Use "In practice, that means" not "That means"
- Give real examples, not generic statements
- Show insight: "I've found that..." or "I understand..."
- Mention business impact where relevant (cost savings, efficiency)

---

### Paragraph 4: Team Development - Part 1 (3-4 sentences)
**Purpose:** Show leadership through adversity or constraint

**Formula:**
- State a challenging leadership scenario (budget cuts, COVID, rapid growth)
- Describe the shift you made (from X to Y)
- Quantify or describe the impact

**Example:**
> Team development has been a [consistent focus/long-standing interest] of mine. When I [challenging scenario], I [what you changed]. The [effect/result] made a substantial impact on [outcome].

**Tips:**
- Use "has been" not "has always been"
- Be specific about the context (budget cuts, layoffs, pandemic)
- Focus on mindset/culture shifts, not just process
- Avoid: "I've consistently inherited environments and improved them" (too boastful)

---

### Paragraph 5: Team Development - Part 2 (3-4 sentences)
**Purpose:** Show ongoing team development with specific examples

**Formula:**
- State your approach to growing team skills
- Give 2 specific before/after examples
- End with your philosophy or approach

**Example:**
> With my current [team type], I have [your approach]. As a result, for example, [specific person's progression]. Additionally, [second person's progression]. I [your philosophy about growth/development].

**Tips:**
- Use "I have" not "I've" for formal tone
- Give concrete examples with role progressions
- End with a statement about your approach to development
- Avoid vague statements like "helped them grow"

---

### Paragraph 6: Company Culture/Values Alignment (2-3 sentences)
**Purpose:** Show you researched them and align with their values

**Formula:**
- Reference specific leadership principles or company values
- Connect to how you work
- Optional: Mention relevant technology interests

**Example:**
> The leadership principles your company highlights—"[Principle 1]" and "[Principle 2]"—align with how I approach [your domain]. [How you embody those principles]. Your investment in [company focus] resonates strongly with my interests, as well. Specifically, [relevant example].

**Tips:**
- Quote their exact leadership principles/values
- Don't use: "describe exactly how I work", "resonates with"
- Use: "align with how I approach", "resonates strongly with my interests, as well"
- Be specific about why it resonates (give example)

---

### Paragraph 7: Address Gaps (2-3 sentences)
**Purpose:** Proactively address obvious mismatches

**Formula:**
- Directly state the gap
- Explain why it's transferable or minor
- Show you understand the underlying skills are the same

**Example:**
> One gap to address: my platform experience centers on [Your Platform] rather than [Their Platform]. That said, the fundamental skills involved in both platforms are easily transferable: [list skills]. While the platforms differ, the problems [they solve/each have] are similar.

**Tips:**
- Be direct: "One gap to address:"
- Don't be defensive or over-explain
- Show you understand the transferable fundamentals
- Avoid: "I'm confident my experience applies" (sounds like resume template)
- Use: "the fundamental skills are transferable", "the problems remain similar"

---

### Paragraph 8: Closing (1-2 sentences)
**Purpose:** Professional close that invites dialogue

**Formula:**
- Thank them for their time
- Express openness to discussion
- Keep it professional but warm

**Example:**
> Thank you for taking the time to consider me for this position. I welcome the opportunity to discuss whether I would be the right fit for [Company]'s needs.

**Tips:**
- Use "I welcome" not "I'd welcome" for formal tone
- Frame as mutual evaluation: "whether I would be the right fit"
- Avoid: "Let's talk", "I look forward to hearing from you"

## Voice & Tone Guidelines

### DO:
- ✅ Use formal contractions sparingly (only in "I've found" type insights)
- ✅ Use "I have [past tense]" for accomplishments
- ✅ Use "has been" not "has always been"
- ✅ Give specific examples with context
- ✅ Show insight: "I've found that...", "I understand..."
- ✅ Be direct about gaps: "One gap to address:"
- ✅ Use em-dashes (—) for parenthetical thoughts
- ✅ Write like you're explaining to a colleague, but in a formal email

### DON'T:
- ❌ Use AI tells: "I'm excited", "thrilled", "for a specific reason"
- ❌ Use buzzwords: "platform-agnostic", "synergy", "game-changer"
- ❌ Use resume language: "proven track record of", "demonstrated ability to"
- ❌ Over-explain obvious things: "The business runs entirely on technology"
- ❌ Use declarative statements: "That's exactly how I work"
- ❌ Use tutorial voice: "That means... It also means..."
- ❌ Be too casual: contractions everywhere, informal language
- ❌ Generic praise: "I've consistently improved environments"

### Formality Balance:
**Too Formal (Resume Voice):**
> "I'm confident my experience architecting SSO federation, SCIM provisioning automation, and conditional access policies applies directly to any IDP ecosystem."

**Too Casual (Text Message):**
> "But the core work—identity governance, SSO integrations, conditional access, automated provisioning—transfers across platforms."

**Just Right (Professional Email):**
> "That said, the fundamental skills involved in both platforms are easily transferable: identity governance, SSO architecture, conditional access, and automated provisioning are nearly identical."

## Length Guidelines

**Total:** 400-450 words (3/4 page single-spaced)
**Paragraphs:**
- Opening: 2-3 sentences
- Body paragraphs: 3-4 sentences each
- Closing: 1-2 sentences

**Too short:** Looks like you didn't put in effort
**Too long:** Won't get read fully

## Customization Checklist

Before sending, verify you've customized:
- [ ] Company name (appears 3-4 times)
- [ ] Job title (appears 2-3 times)
- [ ] Specific leadership principles/values from their website
- [ ] Specific company focus or technology investment
- [ ] Gap analysis (platform, industry, company type)
- [ ] Technical examples match their stack where possible
- [ ] Examples align with their job requirements

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Copy-paste obvious** - Change more than just company name
2. **Generic opening** - Reference something specific from job posting
3. **Resume repetition** - Don't just restate resume bullets
4. **Explaining the obvious** - They know what their company does
5. **Defensive tone** - Address gaps directly, don't over-explain
6. **AI tells** - Read it out loud; does it sound like you'd say it?
7. **Too many contractions** - Sounds too casual
8. **No contractions** - Sounds robotic
9. **Buzzword soup** - Use plain language
10. **Generic closing** - Make it about mutual fit
